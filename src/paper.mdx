## Abstract
<div class="text-justify text-base leading-7">
Multi-agent AI offers potential for complex reasoning, yet current collaboration mechanisms often lack coordination, transparency, and accountability. In this work, we introduce Agentic Courtroom Orchestration (ACO), a judicially inspired multi-agent framework that organizes visual reasoning around evidence collection, adversarial argumentation, impartial judgment, and collective voting. In ACO, visual expert agents extract explicit facts; prosecutor and attorney agents debate candidate interpretations under a presumption-of-innocence principle; a judge evaluates competing opinions for factual grounding and consistency; and juries aggregate decisions across reasoning paths and backbone models. Evaluated across seven visual reasoning tasks, ACO consistently outperforms vanilla inference, achieving 35.9%--48.4% relative gains in accuracy. Stage-level analysis shows that evidence-grounded deliberation delivers the primary performance gains, while the judge stage refines reasoning quality and jury aggregation improves robustness. Task-level analysis reveals a division of responsibility: facts-driven tasks, such as Counting and Object Recognition, rely primarily on early visual evidence, whereas reasoning-intensive tasks, such as Spatial Reasoning and Navigation, benefit more from adjudicating competing interpretations. Together, these findings suggest that democratic AI provides a principled route to shared intelligence, in which heterogeneous agents can coordinate through transparent, accountable, and value-aligned forms of collaboration rather than merely aggregating isolated outputs.
