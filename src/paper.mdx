## Abstract
<div class="text-justify text-base leading-7">
  Artificial intelligence is rapidly shifting from isolated agents toward societies that perceive, deliberate, and decide collectively. Yet shared intelligence remains difficult to trust when evidence is opaque, inter-agent disagreement is unstructured, and decision-making responsibility is diffuse. Human democratic and judicial principles offer a powerful blueprint for organizing collective intelligence. Here we introduce <em>Self-Governed Collective Intelligence</em> (SCI), a paradigm for trustworthy agent societies in multimodal reasoning inspired by human democratic and judicial systems. SCI assigns agents to institutional roles for evidence collection, adversarial deliberation, judicial review, and jury-based aggregation, transforming model disagreement into a structured process with explicit shared values. Evaluated across five multimodal backbone models, seven complex reasoning tasks, and 1,347 cases, SCI consistently improves pass@1 accuracy over standard inference, achieving relative gains of 36.9%–48.5%. Beyond performance gains, SCI reveals how collective reliability can emerge from procedural responsibility among AI agents. Fact-driven tasks, such as counting and object recognition, derive most of their gains (66.7%) from shared evidence, whereas complex reasoning tasks, such as dynamic spatial reasoning and goal-oriented navigation, benefit most from adjudicating competing interpretations (38.4%). By making evidence visible, judgment accountable, and responsibility separable, SCI demonstrates that recursive and responsible reasoning can emerge from collaborative AI agents governed by shared values. This highlights a promising path forward for aligning AI agents with human values to create trustworthy AI societies.
